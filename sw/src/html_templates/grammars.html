Quick intro

An alphabet is a finite set of symbols.
A word is a finite sequence of symbols from given alphabet.
A language is a set of words. It can be finite or infinite

What are formal grammars?

Formal grammars are a way to describe languages. Basically, a language L over the alphabet A can be constructed by starting from a symbol S (S is not a member of A) and then applying a series of transformations (called productions), which change S and the subsequent sequences of symbols into different sequences of symbols (it is just like the find-replace functionality available in most text editors). The process stops once we reach a sequence of symbols which is a member of L. Applying different transformations can produce every word of L.

Formal definition of a formal grammar

A formal grammar is an ordered quadruple (it has 4 elements and we know which is which)
G = (T, N, S, P), T ∩ N = ∅, S ∈ N
where
  • T is the set of terminals (symbols that make up the target alphabet).
  • N is the set of nonterminals (symbols that do not appear in the final word, but are present during its creation).
  • S is the head of the grammar (the nonterminal that starts all word generation).
  • P is the set of productions. Productions have the form of LHS -> RHS, where LHS is a sequence of nonterminals and RHS is a sequence of nonterminals and terminals.
A grammar is said to be context free if (and only if) every production contains a single nonterminal as its LHS.

The Backus-Naur Form

The Backus-Naur From is a standardized way to write the production rules. All nonterminals are wrapped in diamond braces. LHS and RHS of a production are separated by ::= . The alternative operator, | , introduces an alternative RHS to the given LHS.
